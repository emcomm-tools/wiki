What is a Net?

A net (network) is an organized on-air meeting of amateur radio operators. One station acts as Net Control Station (NCS) and directs all communications. Nets provide:

  • Order - Structured communication prevents chaos
  • Efficiency - Messages flow through a central point
  • Accountability - NCS tracks who's present and what traffic moves
  • Training - Regular practice builds skills for emergencies

Types of Nets

TypePurposeFormat
Traffic Net Pass formal written messages (radiograms) Directed, formal
Emergency Net Handle disaster/emergency communications Directed, strict discipline
Training Net Practice procedures, train new operators Directed, educational
Public Service Net Support events (marathons, parades) Directed or tactical
Social/Ragchew Net Casual conversation, fellowship Informal, round-table
Swap Net Buy/sell/trade equipment Informal
Technical Net Q&A on technical topics Semi-formal

Net Formats

  • Directed Net: All communication goes through NCS. You must be recognized before transmitting. Used for traffic and emergency nets.
  • Open/Informal Net: Stations may call each other directly. NCS maintains order but doesn't control every exchange.
  • Round-Table: Stations take turns in order. Common for ragchew nets.
  • Tactical Net: Uses tactical callsigns (e.g., "Command", "Aid Station 1"). Common for public service events.

  • Open and close the net
  • Call for check-ins
  • Maintain the station roster
  • Recognize stations and direct traffic
  • Keep the net moving efficiently
  • Handle interference and problems
  • Ensure all traffic is passed

üìù Net Liaison

Connects this net to other nets. Responsibilities:

  • Check into multiple nets
  • Carry traffic between nets
  • Relay information between organizations

üë• Net Participants

Regular stations checking into the net:

  • Check in when called
  • Wait to be recognized before transmitting
  • Handle traffic as requested
  • Monitor the frequency
  • Follow NCS instructions

How to Check In

Basic Check-In Procedure

NCS: This net is now open for check-ins. Stations with emergency or priority traffic, call now. [pause for emergency traffic] NCS: Stations with routine traffic, call now. YOU: W1ABC WITH TRAFFIC NCS: W1ABC, welcome to the net. What traffic do you have? YOU: One routine for Hartford. NCS: Roger, W1ABC, one routine for Hartford. Stand by. [NCS notes your traffic] NCS: Stations without traffic, call now. OTHER: W2XYZ OTHER: W3DEF MOBILE

Check-In Elements

  • Callsign: Always required
  • Traffic: "WITH TRAFFIC" or "NO TRAFFIC"
  • Status: Mobile, portable, QRP, relay, etc.
  • Location: If relevant (especially EmComm)
  • Comments: Brief - "checking in short time only"
Tip: Keep check-ins SHORT. Just your call and essential info. Save the ragchewing for later!

Check-In Timing

  1. Wait for NCS to call for check-ins
  2. Listen to make sure frequency is clear
  3. Key up and give your call clearly
  4. Unkey and wait for acknowledgment
  5. Don't double - if two stations call together, NCS will sort it out

Sample Net Scripts

Formal Net Opening

NCS: Calling the [NET NAME]. Calling the [NET NAME]. This is [CALLSIGN], net control for this session of the [NET NAME], meeting on [FREQUENCY] at [TIME]. This is a directed net. Please do not transmit without being recognized by net control. The purpose of this net is [STATE PURPOSE]. Is there any station with emergency or priority traffic? [Pause - handle any emergency traffic immediately] Are there any stations with traffic? Stations with traffic, call now. [Take check-ins, note traffic] Are there any stations without traffic wishing to check in? [Take remaining check-ins] We have [NUMBER] stations checked in with [NUMBER] pieces of traffic. Let's begin passing traffic.

Directing Traffic

NCS: W1ABC, you have traffic for Hartford. W2XYZ, can you take Hartford traffic? W2XYZ: W2XYZ, affirmative. NCS: W1ABC, call W2XYZ and pass your traffic. W1ABC: W2XYZ, W1ABC. W2XYZ: W1ABC, W2XYZ, ready to copy. [Traffic is passed - see Radiogram article] W1ABC: W1ABC back to net. W2XYZ: W2XYZ back to net, traffic received. NCS: Roger, W1ABC and W2XYZ. Thank you. Next, W3DEF has traffic for Boston...

Formal Net Closing

NCS: Are there any late check-ins? [Take any late arrivals] NCS: Is there any further business for this net? [Handle any remaining items] NCS: Hearing none, this concludes tonight's session of the [NET NAME]. We had [NUMBER] check-ins and passed [NUMBER] pieces of traffic. Thank you all for participating. This net meets every [DAY] at [TIME] on this frequency. This is [CALLSIGN] closing the net and returning the frequency to normal amateur use. 73.

Handling Traffic

When You Have Traffic to Send

  1. Check in "with traffic" and state destination
  2. Wait for NCS to pair you with a receiving station
  3. When directed, call the receiving station
  4. Pass your message using proper format
  5. Get acknowledgment
  6. Return to net: "[CALL] back to net"

When You Can Receive Traffic

  1. When NCS asks "Can anyone take [CITY] traffic?" - respond
  2. Be ready to copy before you volunteer
  3. Copy the message accurately
  4. Acknowledge receipt: "Roger, number [XX]"
  5. Return to net
  6. Deliver or relay the message
See Also: Radiogram & Message Handling for detailed message format and procedures.

Tactical Callsigns

During events, tactical calls identify function, not person:

  • Net Control - NCS position
  • Command - EOC or incident commander
  • Shelter 1, Shelter 2 - Shelter locations
  • Aid 1, Aid 2 - Medical aid stations
  • Mobile 1, Mobile 2 - Roving units
  • Shadow - Official following served agency person
FCC Requirement: You must still ID with your FCC callsign every 10 minutes and at the end of communication, even when using tactical calls.
Proper tactical use:
"Shelter 1, Net Control."
"Net Control, Shelter 1, go ahead."
"Shelter 1, requesting status update."
"Net Control, Shelter 1, currently have 45 evacuees, all stable. Shelter 1, W1ABC."
(FCC call given at end)

Tips for Success

‚úÖ DO

  • Listen before transmitting
  • Keep transmissions brief
  • Speak clearly and slowly
  • Use phonetics for unclear words
  • Wait for acknowledgment
  • Log everything
  • Practice regularly

‚ùå DON'T

  • Transmit without being recognized
  • Use "CB lingo" (10-4, good buddy)
  • Argue on the air
  • Key up before ready to speak
  • Forget your callsign
  • Make assumptions - ask if unsure
  • Leave frequency without telling NCS

üìù As NCS

  • Be prepared and organized
  • Be fair and patient
  • Keep the net moving
  • Don't let one station dominate
  • Summarize often
  • Ask for fills when conditions poor
  • Thank participants

üéØ Getting Better

  • Check into nets regularly
  • Volunteer to handle traffic
  • Offer to be assistant NCS
  • Take NCS training
  • Join ARES/RACES
  • Participate in exercises
  • Learn from experienced ops
