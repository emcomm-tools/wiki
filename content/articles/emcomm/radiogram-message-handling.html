Radiogram & NTS Message Handling

The ARRL Radiogram is the standard format for passing formal written traffic in amateur radio. Used by the National Traffic System (NTS) and emergency communications networks, proper message handling ensures accurate, reliable delivery of critical information.

Overview

The National Traffic System (NTS) was created by ARRL to handle routine and emergency message traffic. The radiogram format provides:

  • Standardization - Everyone uses the same format
  • Accuracy - Structured fields reduce errors
  • Accountability - Messages can be tracked and verified
  • Efficiency - Trained operators handle traffic quickly
Why This Matters: In a real emergency, a garbled message could send rescuers to the wrong location or cause critical supplies to go elsewhere. Proper message handling saves lives.

Radiogram Format

Every radiogram has four parts:

  1. Preamble - Message metadata (number, precedence, origin, date, etc.)
  2. Address - Who the message is going to
  3. Text - The actual message content (25 words or less recommended)
  4. Signature - Who the message is from

The Preamble

The preamble contains all the routing and tracking information:

FieldDescriptionExample
Number Sequential message number assigned by originating station. Start at 1 each year. 47
Precedence Priority level: R, W, P, or EMERGENCY R
HX Handling instructions (optional). See HX codes below. HXC
Station of Origin Callsign of station that created the message W1AW
Check Word count of the TEXT only (not address or signature) 12
Place of Origin City and state where message originated NEWINGTON CT
Time Filed Time message was created (optional, 24-hour UTC) 1830Z
Date Date message was created JAN 8

Precedence Levels

CodeNameUse
E EMERGENCY Life or death situation. Any station may interrupt any net to handle. Must be cleared before all other traffic. Rare - most "emergencies" are actually Priority.
P PRIORITY Important messages with time limit: official messages, evacuation notices, welfare traffic in disaster areas, press traffic. Handle before Routine.
W WELFARE Relates to health/welfare of individuals in disaster area OR inquiries about same. Handle before Routine but after Priority.
R ROUTINE Normal traffic. Greetings, test messages, most day-to-day NTS traffic. No urgency.
Do Not Overuse EMERGENCY: True EMERGENCY precedence is extremely rare. Using it inappropriately disrupts the system and desensitizes operators. When in doubt, use PRIORITY.

Handling Instructions (HX Codes)

Optional instructions that tell receiving stations how to handle the message:

CodeMeaning
HXA Collect delivery authorized by addressee within ___ miles. (Insert miles.)
HXB Cancel message if not delivered within ___ hours of filing time.
HXC Report date and time of delivery to originating station.
HXD Report to originating station the identity of station that delivered message.
HXE Delivering station get reply from addressee, originate message back.
HXF Hold delivery until ___ (date/time).
HXG Delivery by mail or landline (telephone) OK. Do not phone after 10 PM local.
Most Common: HXG (delivery by phone OK) and HXC (report delivery) are used most frequently. Many routine messages use no HX code at all.

Address Block

The address must be complete enough to deliver the message:

  • Line 1: Name (and callsign if amateur)
  • Line 2: Street address or PO Box
  • Line 3: City, State, ZIP
  • Line 4: Telephone number (with area code)
JOHN SMITH W1ABC
123 MAIN STREET
NEWINGTON CT 06111
860 555 1234

Address Counting: The address is NOT included in the word count (check).

Telephone Numbers in Address

Phone numbers are written as groups without dashes or parentheses:

  • Spoken: "EIGHT SIX ZERO" (pause) "FIVE FIVE FIVE" (pause) "ONE TWO THREE FOUR"
  • Written: 860 555 1234

Message Text

Guidelines

  • 25 words or less - Keep it concise. Longer messages should be split.
  • All text characters - No punctuation except X for period, R for "received"
  • Use X for period - "ARRIVED SAFELY X CALL WHEN YOU CAN X"
  • Spell out numbers - "THREE" not "3" (unless using figures intentionally)
  • No contractions - "DO NOT" not "DON'T"
  • Initial groups - Count as one word: "ARRL" = 1 word
  • Mixed groups - Letters and numbers together = 1 word: "W1AW" = 1 word

The Check Must Be Accurate! If sender says "CHECK 12" and receiver counts 13 words, there's an error. Always verify the count and request fills if needed.

Signature

The signature identifies who the message is FROM. It can be:

  • A name: "UNCLE BOB"
  • A name and callsign: "JOHN W1ABC"
  • An organization: "COUNTY EOC"
  • Multiple lines if needed

The signature is NOT included in the word count.

ARL Numbered Radiogram Messages

ARL codes are pre-defined messages that save time. Say "ARL FIFTY" and the receiving station writes the standard text. Use for common situations.

Most Common ARL Messages

ARL THREEAm in ___ hospital. Receiving excellent care and recovering fine.
ARL FOUROnly slight property damage here. Do not be concerned about disaster reports.
ARL FIVEAm moving to new address. Will inform you of new address when relocated.
ARL SIXWill contact you as soon as possible.

ARL EIGHTNeed additional ___ mobile or portable equipment for relief work. Please ship immediately.
ARL NINEAdditional ___ radio operators needed to operate relief net. Please send volunteers.
ARL TENPlease contact ___. Advise to standby and provide further emergency information, instructions or assistance.

Counting ARL: "ARL FIFTY" counts as 2 words in the check. "ARL SIXTY SEVEN" counts as 3 words.

Sending a Radiogram

Voice Procedure

// Net Control calls for traffic NCS: Any traffic for the net? YOU: W1AW, one routine for Newington. NCS: W1AW, call W1XYZ for your traffic. // Move to working frequency or stay on net YOU: W1XYZ, W1AW, ready to copy? W1XYZ: W1AW, ready to copy, go ahead. // Send preamble YOU: NUMBER FOUR SEVEN ROUTINE W ONE A W CHECK ONE TWO NEWINGTON CONNECTICUT JANUARY EIGHT BREAK // Send address YOU: JOHN SMITH ONE TWO THREE MAIN STREET ANYTOWN NEW YORK ONE TWO THREE FOUR FIVE FIVE ONE EIGHT (pause) FIVE FIVE FIVE (pause) ONE TWO THREE FOUR BREAK // Send text YOU: HAPPY BIRTHDAY X HOPE YOU HAVE A WONDERFUL DAY X LOOKING FORWARD TO SEEING YOU SOON X BREAK // Send signature YOU: UNCLE BOB END NO MORE W1XYZ: ROGER, NUMBER FOUR SEVEN, W1AW.

Key Prowords When Sending

  • BREAK - Separates preamble/address/text/signature
  • I SPELL - About to spell phonetically
  • FIGURES - Numbers follow, written as digits
  • INITIAL - Single letter follows
  • END - Message complete
  • MORE - I have more messages
  • NO MORE - Last message

Receiving a Radiogram

Before You Start

  • Have blank radiogram forms ready (or paper)
  • Have pen/pencil ready
  • Confirm you're ready: "Ready to copy"

During Reception

  • Write everything down immediately
  • Count words as you copy the text
  • Don't interrupt unless you miss something

Requesting Fills

If you miss something, request a "fill" using these phrases:

  • SAY AGAIN - Repeat entire message
  • SAY AGAIN ___ - Repeat the word ___
  • WORD AFTER ___ - What comes after ___?
  • WORD BEFORE ___ - What comes before ___?
  • SAY AGAIN ALL AFTER ___ - Repeat everything after ___
  • SAY AGAIN ALL BEFORE ___ - Repeat everything before ___
  • SAY AGAIN ___ TO ___ - Repeat from ___ to ___

After Reception

  1. Verify word count - Does your count match the check?
  2. Roger the message - "ROGER NUMBER 47"
  3. Book the message - Add your callsign and date/time received to your log
  4. Deliver or relay - Get the message to its destination

Tips for Accuracy

  1. Read back unclear portions - If unsure, confirm with sender
  2. Use phonetics for unusual words - Spell them out
  3. Always verify the check - Count is critical
  4. Write legibly - Someone else may deliver your message
  5. Copy EXACTLY what is sent - Don't interpret or "correct"
  6. Keep messages short - 25 words or less is ideal
  7. Use ARL codes - Saves time, reduces errors
  8. Practice regularly - Join NTS nets to build skill
Pro Tip: Keep blank radiogram forms in your go-kit. You can download official ARRL forms at www.arrl.org/nts.
