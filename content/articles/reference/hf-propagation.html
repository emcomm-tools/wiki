HF radio waves can travel long distances by bouncing (refracting) off the ionosphere. This is called sky wave propagation. The ionosphere changes constantly based on:

  • Time of day - Sunlight ionizes the atmosphere
  • Season - Sun angle affects ionization
  • Solar activity - Sunspots, flares, CMEs
  • Frequency - Different bands behave differently

The Ionosphere

LayerHeightPresentEffect on HF
D Layer 50-90 km Day only Absorbs lower HF (160m, 80m). Disappears at night, allowing these bands to open.
E Layer 90-150 km Day (+ sporadic) Short skip (500-1500 km). Sporadic E can open VHF!
F1 Layer 150-250 km Day only Merges with F2 at night. Medium distance.
F2 Layer 250-400 km Day and night Primary layer for long-distance HF. Most important for DX.

Key Concepts

  • MUF (Maximum Usable Frequency) - Highest frequency that will refract back. Above MUF, signals pass through to space.
  • LUF (Lowest Usable Frequency) - Lowest frequency usable. Below LUF, absorption is too high.
  • Skip Zone - Area between ground wave and first sky wave return where no signal is heard.
  • Skip Distance - Distance to first sky wave return point.

Band-by-Band Guide

160 Meters (1.8 MHz) - "Top Band"

Best: Night, winter, low solar activity

Poor: Daytime (D-layer absorption), summer static

  • Primarily ground wave (50-100 miles)
  • Night: regional sky wave (500-1000 miles)
  • Very susceptible to atmospheric noise
  • Winter nights best for DX

80 Meters (3.5 MHz) - Regional Workhorse

Best: Night (regional nets), NVIS daytime

Poor: Summer daytime, high noise

  • Day: NVIS 0-300 miles, heavy absorption limits DX
  • Night: Opens for regional/continental (500-2000 mi)
  • Primary EmComm band for night operations
  • QRN (static) can be severe in summer

60 Meters (5 MHz) - EmComm Band

Best: Day and night, transitional

Fair: Limited bandwidth channels

  • Excellent for NVIS day and night
  • Less affected by D-layer than 80m
  • Shared with government - follow rules carefully
  • Good emergency interoperability band

40 Meters (7 MHz) - Day/Night Transition

Best: Dawn/dusk, daytime NVIS, night regional

Fair: Crowded, broadcast interference

  • Day: NVIS (regional), some skip
  • Night: Opens for DX (1000-5000 mi)
  • Most versatile HF band for EmComm
  • Works almost 24 hours in some form

30 Meters (10 MHz) - WARC Band

Best: Daytime, transition periods

Fair: No phone allowed, narrow

  • Excellent for digital modes
  • Less crowded (no contests)
  • Good transition band day/night
  • 200W max power limit

20 Meters (14 MHz) - DX Workhorse

Best: Daytime, worldwide DX

Poor: Night (often closes)

  • Primary daytime DX band
  • Often open all day during high solar activity
  • Closes at night (except gray line)
  • First band to check for long-distance

17 Meters (18 MHz) - WARC Band

Best: Daytime, high solar activity

  • Similar to 20m but less crowded
  • No contests
  • Good DX when conditions support

15 Meters (21 MHz) - Solar Dependent

Best: Daytime, solar max years

Poor: Solar minimum - may not open

  • Excellent DX during high sunspot numbers
  • Often dead during solar minimum
  • When open, less crowded than 20m

12 Meters (24 MHz) - WARC Band

Best: Solar max, daytime

Poor: Often closed during solar minimum

  • Follows solar cycle closely
  • No contests, less crowded
  • Great DX when open

10 Meters (28 MHz) - Magic Band Gateway

Best: Solar max, sporadic E season (summer)

Poor: Solar minimum - very quiet

  • Wide open during solar max - worldwide DX
  • Sporadic E openings (even at solar min)
  • Technicians have phone privileges!
  • FM repeaters and simplex (29.6 MHz)

Gray Line Propagation

The gray line (or terminator) is the boundary between day and night on Earth. Enhanced propagation occurs along this line because:

  • D-layer absorption is reduced
  • F-layer is still ionized
  • Signals can travel along the terminator
Gray Line Tip: Check propagation at your local sunrise and sunset for potential DX on bands that are normally challenging.

Solar Conditions

Solar Indices

IndexWhat It MeansGood Values
SFI (Solar Flux) Solar radio emissions at 10.7cm. Higher = better HF propagation 100+ is good, 150+ is excellent
SN (Sunspot Number) Count of sunspots. Correlates with ionization Higher = better high bands
A Index Geomagnetic activity (daily average) Below 10 is quiet, good
K Index Geomagnetic activity (3-hour) 0-2 is quiet, 5+ is storm

Solar Cycle

The sun follows an approximately 11-year cycle:

  • Solar Maximum: Many sunspots, high SFI, excellent high-band propagation (10m, 15m, 12m)
  • Solar Minimum: Few sunspots, low SFI, high bands often dead, rely on 40m/80m
Current Cycle: We are in Solar Cycle 25, which began in December 2019. Check current conditions at spaceweather.com or hamqsl.com/solar.html

Disturbances

EventEffectDuration
Solar Flare Sudden increased D-layer absorption, HF blackout on sunlit side Minutes to hours
CME (Coronal Mass Ejection) Geomagnetic storm 1-3 days later, disrupted propagation Hours to days
Geomagnetic Storm Depressed MUF, auroral absorption, poor HF Hours to days
Proton Event Polar cap absorption, high-latitude paths affected Days
