POTA - Parks on the Air

Activate parks, forests, and public lands. Most popular portable program!

  • Minimum QSOs: 10 for valid activation
  • Modes: Any - SSB, CW, FT8, etc.
  • Bands: Any amateur band
  • Website: pota.app
  • Logging: ADIF upload to POTA site

Park Types

  • National Parks
  • State/Provincial Parks
  • National Forests
  • Wildlife Refuges
  • Historic Sites

SOTA - Summits on the Air

Activate mountain summits. Points based on summit height!

  • Minimum QSOs: 4 for valid activation
  • Activation Zone: Within 25m vertical of summit
  • No motorized access: Must hike/climb
  • Website: sota.org.uk / sotawatch.sota.org.uk
  • Power: Must be portable (battery/solar)

Points

  • 1 point: Lowest valid summits
  • 10 points: High, prominent peaks
  • Winter bonus: Extra points Dec-Mar (North America)

IOTA - Islands on the Air

Activate islands worldwide. Highly sought-after by DXers!

  • Groups: Islands grouped by reference (NA-XXX)
  • Popular: Rare islands draw pileups
  • Website: iota-world.org

Portable Antennas

üé£ End-Fed Half-Wave (EFHW)

Most popular portable HF antenna. One support point, easy setup.

  • Length: Half wavelength (e.g., 20m = 10m wire)
  • Matching: 49:1 unun transformer
  • Multi-band: Works on harmonics (40/20/10m)
  • Setup: Sloper, inverted-V, or horizontal
  • Pros: Simple, one feedpoint, no radials
  • Cons: RF on feedline, needs choke

üîó Linked Dipole

Resonant dipole with plugs to change bands.

  • Design: Full-size dipole with links for band change
  • Efficiency: Excellent (no lossy matching)
  • Setup: Needs center support + two ends
  • Pros: Most efficient, no tuner needed
  • Cons: Band change requires adjustment

üéã Vertical with Radials

Ground-mounted or elevated vertical.

  • Types: Telescoping whip, loaded, trapped
  • Radials: 2-4 elevated, or more ground radials
  • Pros: Low angle (DX), omni-directional
  • Cons: Needs counterpoise, setup time

üîÅ Magnetic Loop

Small, efficient loop for restricted spaces.

  • Size: Typically 3-4 feet diameter
  • Tuning: Narrow bandwidth, must retune
  • Pros: Quiet RX, small footprint, stealth
  • Cons: Narrow bandwidth, expensive, fragile

Antenna Supports

  • Telescoping mast: Fiberglass fishing poles (Jackite, SOTAbeams)
  • Trees: Throw line/weight to hang wire
  • Tripod: Camera tripod for vertical base
  • Vehicle: Mount to car with mag-mount or drive-on
  • Hiking poles: Improvised mast

Site Selection Tips

  • Elevation helps - Higher = better signal
  • Clear horizons - Especially toward population centers
  • Trees for supports - But not blocking too much
  • Shade - Summer sun is brutal on operator and gear
  • Away from RFI - Distance from power lines, buildings
  • Comfort - Good seat, flat ground, wind shelter

Operating Tips

Getting Spotted

  • Self-spot: Use POTA app or SOTA spotting
  • Include frequency, mode, and park/summit
  • Hunters will find you! - Spots bring pileups

Pileup Management

  • Work by call area: "Sevens only" then "Eights"
  • Work by suffix: "Stations ending in Alpha"
  • Keep it moving: Short exchanges, fast logging
  • Stay calm: Pileups are fun but can be hectic!
