What Are Amateur Satellites?

Amateur radio satellites (often called "OSCAR" - Orbiting Satellite Carrying Amateur Radio) are satellites built and operated by amateur radio operators for amateur communications. They typically carry:

  • FM Repeaters - Simple cross-band repeaters in space
  • Linear Transponders - Translate a segment of spectrum
  • Digital Payloads - Digipeaters, store-and-forward
  • Beacons - Telemetry and propagation experiments

Types of Satellites

üìª FM Satellites (Easy Mode)

Cross-band FM repeaters in orbit. Easiest way to start!

ModeFM Voice
EquipmentDual-band HT
DifficultyBeginner

Characteristics

  • Work like a repeater - one signal at a time
  • Can work with a handheld radio and small antenna
  • Very popular, can be crowded during passes
  • Short QSOs (callsign, grid, signal report)

üìä Linear Transponder Satellites

Translate an entire band segment - multiple QSOs simultaneously.

ModeSSB/CW
EquipmentAll-mode radio + yagi
DifficultyIntermediate

Characteristics

  • Multiple simultaneous QSOs possible
  • Use SSB or CW (low power!)
  • More room for everyone
  • Inverting transponders flip sideband
  • Requires more precise tracking

Orbits & Passes

üåç LEO (Low Earth Orbit)

Most amateur satellites orbit at 400-800 km altitude:

  • Period: ~90-100 minutes per orbit
  • Pass duration: 5-15 minutes visible
  • Passes per day: 4-6 usable passes typical
  • Footprint: ~4000-5000 km diameter

Doppler Shift

Satellites move at ~27,000 km/h! This causes Doppler shift - the frequency changes as the satellite approaches and recedes.

Doppler Rule of Thumb: For FM sats, tune your DOWNLINK receiver during the pass - start high, end low. Most tracking software shows the corrected frequency.

Equipment Needed

üî∞ Beginner (FM Sats)

  • Dual-band HT (5W+)
  • Dual-band antenna (Arrow, Elk)
  • Full duplex helpful but not required

Cost: $150-400

üì° Intermediate

  • All-mode transceiver
  • Yagi antennas (2m + 70cm)
  • Antenna rotator (AZ/EL)
  • Full duplex capability

Cost: $500-2000

üöÄ Advanced

  • Satellite-specific transceiver (IC-9700)
  • High-gain crossed yagis
  • Computer-controlled rotator
  • SDR for Doppler

Cost: $2000+

Essential: The Arrow Antenna

The Arrow II (or similar) is the go-to antenna for portable FM satellite work:

  • Handheld dual-band yagi (3el 2m / 7el 70cm)
  • Built-in diplexer for single feedline
  • Lightweight, breaks down for transport
  • Point at satellite and track manually

TLE (Two-Line Elements)

Tracking software needs orbital data called TLE or Keplerian elements:

  • Update TLEs regularly (weekly minimum)
  • AMSAT provides amateur satellite TLEs
  • CelesTrak is another good source
  • Outdated TLEs = incorrect pass predictions

Operating Procedures

FM Satellite Etiquette

  • Keep it SHORT - Callsign, grid, signal report only
  • Listen first - Don't call over ongoing QSOs
  • Low power - 5W is usually plenty
  • Don't kerchunk - Always ID when you transmit
  • Share the pass - One QSO, then let others in
  • Full duplex helps - Hear yourself, know you're hitting it

Linear Transponder Etiquette

  • Very low power - Start with 5-10W, often less needed
  • Find your own downlink first - Before calling CQ
  • Don't be an "alligator" - All mouth, no ears
  • Use LSB on inverting transponders - Comes out USB
  • Spread out - Don't all pile on same frequency
DON'T OVER-POWER! Satellites have very sensitive receivers. Running too much power causes AGC compression that affects everyone. Start with minimum power - you can always increase.

Your First Satellite QSO

Step-by-Step (FM Satellite)

  1. Check satellite status - Verify it's operational
  2. Find a good pass - 30¬∞+ max elevation
  3. Program frequencies - Uplink and downlink
  4. Set CTCSS if needed - SO-50 needs 67.0 Hz
  5. Go outside - Clear view of sky
  6. Aim antenna at AOS point - Where satellite rises
  7. Listen on downlink - Tune for Doppler
  8. When you hear activity:
    • Wait for a gap
    • "VA2OPS FN35"
    • Listen for response
  9. Complete QSO: Exchange callsigns, grids, reports
  10. Log it! - Note satellite, time, grid worked
First Timer Tip: Try listening-only on your first few passes. Learn how QSOs sound, practice tracking, get comfortable before transmitting.

Sample FM Satellite QSO

VA2OPS: "VA2OPS FN35" W1ABC: "VA2OPS W1ABC FN42" VA2OPS: "W1ABC 59 FN35" W1ABC: "59 FN42 73" VA2OPS: "73" Total time: ~15 seconds. That's normal!
